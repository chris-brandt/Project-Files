The Beginning

In the beginning, the Universe existed as a single point. This point was the only thing that "was". It was infinitly small and infinitly dense. The first thing that ever happened was The Big Bang. In an uncalculably small amount of time, the Universe expanded from a point to a three dimensional area, releasing unfathomable amounts of energy and matter.

The First Moment

In the first moment of existence, a mere picosecond... a lot happened. To start, the laws of physics began their rule. First came gravity, the atractive force between all matter. Next were the strong, weak and electromagnetic interactions. The strong interacton is the force holding together subatomic particles and the nuclei of their atoms. The weak interaction is responsible for the radioactive decay of atoms. The electromagnetic interaction is the force created by the interactons of charged particles. Much of the happenings in this moment are unreacheable by our scientists and may never be truly understood.

The Early Universe

The next stage in the Universe's life lasted for about the 377,000 years. First the various subatomic particles like quarks and photons were formed. These particles formed in almost equal amounts of matter and antimatter, which would annihilate each other quickly. In the end matter won out and became abundant. As the Universe cooled it allowed the formation of the composite subatomic particles, our protons and neutrons. As the Universe was still immensely hot, all of the neutrons fused with some of the protons to form Deuterium, which then all fused to form Helium-4. At around the 377,00 year mark, the Universe has cooled enough for atoms to reach their ground state. At this moment each atom releases its energy, forming what we call the Cosmic Background Radiation.

The Dark Ages and Large-Scale Structure Emergence

Like the name implies, the Dark Ages lacked light. The only photons around till about 200 million years were those of the cosmic background radiation. After this point the gas clouds of hydrogen started to collapse into the first stars, which in time formed the first galaxies. Over time, galaxy clusters and superclusters conglomerate and the Universe starts to look like it does today.

The Milky Way

Our galaxy began to form approximatly 12 billion years ago from the stars and gas of preexisting small galaxies in the local Universe. The Milky Way has been around for a long time, and now contains upwards of 400 billion stars and 100 billion planets making it nearly 200,000 light-years wide. At its center lies a supermassive black hole refered to as Sagittarius A, which is about 27,000 light-years from our solar system.

Our Solar System

The solar system in which we live formed 4.6 billion years ago with the gravitational collapse of a giant intersteller molecular cloud. This formed the Sun, the star at the center of the Solar System. There then formed nine planets around the Sun, in order of distance from the sun: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus and Neptune. The first four planets are classified as the Terrestrial Planets because of their rocky and metalalic composition. The outer four are classified as the Gas Giants because their composition and massive size.

Earth

Earth is what we call the planet on which we are standing. It formed 4.5 billion years ago from resultant material in the solar system. Earth has seen a few changes in its history. The first and most striking change was caused by the impact of a dwarf planet not long after Earth's formation. This impact threw material into Earth's orbit, which condensed into the Moon, Earth's lonely satelite. Later, after many impacts of asteroids, comets and protoplanets carrying water, oceans formed on the surface, sheltered by an atmosphere of greenhouse gases allowing the water to stay liquid. About 4 billion years ago, the first living molecules formed on the primordial Earth. Another 2 billion years later, the earliest common ancestor of all current life arose. The next astronomically signifacant event was the first lifeform to leave the Earth's atmosphere and enter orbit. This was accomplished by Yuri Gagarin of the Soviet Union on April 12, 1961.

The Future

Now that we are up to date, we can only speculate on what will happen next. Scientists have a general idea of how it's going to go. Our Universe is expanding and this expansion has been accelerating. The issue with this is the density of energy in the Universe is decreasing, and faster over time. Eventually, all energy will disperse and the Universe will be dark. This new dark age will be dominated by black holes and an endless vacuum ever expanding into the nothingness beyond. Some even say, the accelerating expansion will become fast enough to rip the very fabric of spacetime. For now, be content with your happy and conscious life on our beautiful blue rock.

    Two or more Stars bound in a gravitational orbit of eachother. Not to be confused with a Galaxy.

  • Black Hole
    A region of spacetime exhibiting gravitational acceleration so strong that nothing—no particles or even electromagnetic radiation such as light—can escape from it. Black Holes are created through the Supernova of a massive star. This is an unrivaled stellar event where an unstable star sheds its outer layers in a massive explosion, then collapses under the extreme gravity of its dense core to form the Black Hole.

Our Relationship with "Space"

This section will be less factual than the previous two, and will be more of a reflection from a humble two legged ape creature on a giant rock hurtling through space. For most of human history, the Cosmos were unexplored and unknown. The Sun, Moon and Stars seemed so close, but were still absolutely unreachable. To the people living in more primative times the Cosmos had a much deeper significance. Many civilizations saw these natural forces as deities to explain what they could not understand. Thinking about what the Sun must have seemed like to prehistoric man reveals an unrealized way of thinking.

Imagine a giant orange fireball in the sky which is the biggest natural light source. However, it goes away for half of the day, leaving behind its white corpse, the Moon, to cast eery shadows over the dark landscape. To early man, deifying these objects is a simple way to rationalize their existence. In early civilization, when farming was available, the Sun was required to grow all crops. Now this thing is required for all food output, it's no wonder many civilizations had gods for cosmic objects including planets, their names even come from the Roman and Greek religious systems.

Today, the heavens have lost their wonder. We know all about the Universe. We know the Sun is just a regular star among trillions. We know all about the expanse of lifeless objects stretching for billions of light-years. As early as the year 1608, we have had telescopes to see and document space around us. In 1949, the Bumper-WAC was the first human-made object to reach space, at an altitude of 393 kilometers. Later in 1957, the Sputnik 1 became the first human-made satelite to orbit the Earth. Finally, in 1962, Yuri Gagarin was the first human to orbit the Earth. Since then there have been countless missions to space. With modern technology and a broadening knowledge base, we now understand most of what is out there. Knowing as much as we do, if there was a deity of the classic type humankind has worshiped throughout it's history, we would have found it in our search beyond. However, we do know that we are very rare, and as of writing this, one of a kind. On no other celestial body has even the slightest trace of DNA been found. Given the vastness of the Universe, us being alone is an improbability, yet we have found nothing. The probability of you being who you are is impercievably small. Just by being alive, you are special. Being a human being may have no greater meaning. However, you are in the only place we know of where things can percieve, and you were lucky enough to be born a human that can feel deep emotion, communicate to form social structures and remember vividly. Just by living a happy life and trying to make those around you feel the same gives meaning to the vast nothingness that surrounds us.
